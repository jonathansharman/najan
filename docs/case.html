Case

Within a stative verb phrase, a subject (sʙᴊ) is something that is in that state. A stative verb phrase may also take a genitive (ɢᴇɴ) argument, which relates that argument to the subject according to the semantics of the verb. The genitive preposition qa ⟨qa⟩ means “with respect to”, “with regard to”, or “in relation to”.

jeqavɪθŋɪkozim
jeqavɪθŋɪkozim
ɪɴᴅɢᴇɴexistsʙᴊIwant
“I want to exist.”

The role of the genitive case in Najan is relatively narrow, reserved for abstract relations where no other case applies. For example, the genitive is distinct from the locative and partitive cases.

An agent (ᴀ) of a dynamic verb phrase is an animate, voluntary cause of the action. A patient (ᴘ) is something that is acted upon or undergoes change as a result of the action.

jecikorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
jecikorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
ɪɴᴅᴀIᴘthesuch-thatbe-sandsᴛᴀᴛʀᴇsfortifyᴘғᴠdestroy
“I destroyed the sand castle (intentionally).”

In contrast to an agent, a cause (ᴄᴀᴜs) is involuntary.

jeslokorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
jeslokorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh

“I destroyed the sand castle (unintentionally).”

A cause can also be inanimate.

The lative (ʟᴀᴛ) case is used for any kind of action to or towards something, in space or time.

The semblative (sᴇᴍʙʟ) case marks something the verb is similar to. The semblative particle kɪ ⟨kɪ⟩ means “as”, “like”, or “in the manner of”.
