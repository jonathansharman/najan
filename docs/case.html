Case

Najan marks case using prepositions, in both prepositional phrases acting as arguments to a verb and in stativized verb phrases using the particle vʊ ⟨vʊ⟩.

Within a stative verb phrase, a subject (sʙᴊ) is something that is in the state described by the verb, and an object (ᴏʙᴊ) is a catch-all argument that relates to the subject according to the particular semantics of the verb. The objective preposition qa ⟨qa⟩ could be translated as “with respect to”.

jeqatoŋɪkogwi
jeqatoŋɪkogwi
ɪɴᴅᴏʙᴊyousʙᴊIbe-behind
“I am behind you.”

An agent (ᴀ) of a dynamic verb phrase is an animate, voluntary cause of the action. A patient (ᴘ) is something that is acted upon or undergoes change as a result of the action.

jecikorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
jecikorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
ɪɴᴅᴀIᴘthesuch-thatbe-sandsᴛᴀᴛʀᴇsfortifyᴘғᴠdestroy
“I destroyed the sand castle (intentionally).”

In contrast to an agent, a cause (ᴄᴀᴜs) is involuntary.

jeslokorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
jeslokorʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
ɪɴᴅᴄᴀᴜsIᴘthesuch-thatbe-sandsᴛᴀᴛʀᴇsfortifyᴘғᴠdestroy
“I destroyed the sand castle (unintentionally).”

A cause can also be inanimate.

jeslogivuculrʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
jeslogivuculrʊginʊganθvʊtcekwonθefleh
ɪɴᴅᴄᴀᴜstheseaᴘthesuch-thatbe-sandsᴛᴀᴛʀᴇsfortifyᴘғᴠdestroy
“The sea destroyed the sand castle.”

A comparison (ᴄᴍᴘʀ) is something the verb is similar to. The comparative particle kɪ ⟨kɪ⟩ often translates to “as”, “like”, or “in the manner of”.

honkɪlecpɪf

“[It] soothes like water.”
